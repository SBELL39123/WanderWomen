MARRAKESH Guide

The best sensory overload

Top Tourist Spots

Marrakesh enchants visitors with its rich history and vibrant culture, featuring must-see sites like the Jardin Majorelle and the Medina of Marrakesh.

Jardin Majorelle

  • A captivating garden featuring a vibrant blue villa, once owned by Yves Saint Laurent, showcasing exotic plants and serene pathways.
    • Learn more

    Medina of Marrakesh

  • A UNESCO World Heritage Site, the Medina is a labyrinth of narrow streets, bustling souks, and historic sites, offering an immersive cultural experience.
    • Learn more

    Koutoubia Mosque

    Renowned for its impressive 77-meter minaret, this mosque is a masterpiece of Almohad architecture and a prominent feature of Marrakesh's skyline. Learn more

    Bahia Palace

    An exquisite 19th-century palace showcasing intricate Moroccan design, with lush gardens and grand courtyards reflecting the opulence of its era. Learn more

    Jemaa el-Fnaa

    The city's main square comes alive, especially in the evening, with street performers, food stalls, and a vibrant atmosphere that captures the essence of Marrakesh's lively spirit. Learn more

    Saadian Tombs

    These tombs, dating back to the Saadian dynasty, are known for their luxurious design and historical significance. Learn more

    Ben Youssef Madrasa

    Once one of the largest Islamic schools in North Africa, this madrasa is celebrated for its stunning architecture and intricate tilework. Learn more

    Hidden Gems

    Marrakesh dazzles with its vibrant souks, historic sites, and lively streets, offering a sensory overload that transports visitors to a time and world so unique.

    Le Jardin Secret

    A beautifully restored historic garden in the heart of the medina, featuring Islamic and Moorish design elements, providing a peaceful retreat from the bustling streets.

    Marrakesh Souks

    A maze of markets offering everything from spices and textiles to jewelry and pottery, providing a sensory overload and a shopper's paradise.

    Atlas Mountains

    A short drive from Marrakesh, these mountains provide breathtaking landscapes, traditional Berber villages, and opportunities for hiking and exploring rural life.

    Lalla Takerkoust

    Just 30 km from Marrakesh, this trendy spot features a picturesque lake and a variety of adventure activities, including quad biking, camel rides, and paragliding.

    Cultural Encounters

    Delve into Marrakesh's rich heritage by exploring its bustling souks and experiencing the Berber culture that permeates the city.

    Language

    Arabic and Berber are Morocco's official languages, with French widely spoken in urban areas.

    Music

    Moroccan music is diverse, featuring styles like Andalusian, Gnaoua, and Al-Shaabi, reflecting the country's rich cultural tapestry.

    Climate

    Marrakesh experiences a hot semi-arid climate, with scorching summers and mild winters.

    Clothing Styles

    Traditional attire includes long, loose-fitting garments like djellabas and kaftans, suitable for the hot climate.

    Berber People

    The Berbers, or Amazigh, are Morocco's indigenous inhabitants, known for their unique language, traditions, and crafts.

    Argan Oil

    Produced primarily by women's cooperatives in southwestern Morocco, argan oil is prized for its cosmetic and culinary uses.

    Architecture

    Marrakesh showcases stunning examples of Islamic architecture, including the Koutoubia Mosque and Bahia Palace.

    Souks

    The city's bustling markets offer a sensory feast of spices, textiles, and handcrafted goods.

    Respecting Prayer Times

    In Morocco, the call to prayer (adhan) resonates from mosques five times daily, marking significant moments for reflection and community gathering. These prayer times are integral to Moroccan life and culture. Being mindful of prayer times not only shows respect for local customs but also enriches your cultural experience in Morocco.

    Gastronomic Delights

    Marrakesh's culinary scene is a feast for the senses, blending traditional Moroccan flavors with modern twists, offering a variety of delectable dishes. *All can be veganized or vegetarianized*

    To Eat

    • Tagine:

      A slow-cooked stew prepared in a traditional conical pot, tagine combines meats, seafood, or vegetables with a blend of spices, dried fruits, and nuts, offering a rich and aromatic flavor profile.

    • Couscous:

      Made from steamed semolina wheat granules, couscous is a staple side dish in Moroccan cuisine, often served with stews or salads, and can be prepared in various ways, including sweet versions with dried fruits and nuts.

    • Moroccan Salads:

      Moroccan salads are diverse, ranging from fresh tomato and cucumber salads to cooked dishes like caramelized carrots and roasted peppers, typically seasoned with herbs and spices, and served as appetizers or side dishes.

    • Dates:

      Dates are a common and cherished fruit in Morocco, often enjoyed as a snack, used in desserts, or incorporated into savory dishes, reflecting the country's agricultural heritage.

    • Orange Juice:

      Freshly squeezed orange juice is a popular beverage in Morocco, especially in Marrakesh, where the abundance of citrus groves ensures a sweet and refreshing drink.

    • Mint Tea:

      Known as "Moroccan whisky," this sweet green tea infused with fresh mint is a symbol of hospitality and is traditionally served to guests in ornate glasses.

    Where to Stay

    Staying in a riad offers an authentic experience of Marrakesh's unique architecture and hospitality, providing a tranquil retreat amidst the city's vibrant life.

    Hotel Atlas

    Located in Marrakesh's historic Medina, Hotel Atlas is just a 2-minute stroll from the iconic Jemaa el-Fnaa square. This riad combines traditional Moroccan décor with modern comforts, featuring air-conditioned rooms, some with private bathrooms, and a rooftop terrace offering panoramic city views.

    Hôtel Résidence Amalou

    Situated in the Gueliz district, Hôtel Résidence Amalou offers comfortable accommodations with amenities like air conditioning, satellite TV, and an outdoor pool. Guests can enjoy a buffet breakfast and easy access to local attractions, with the vibrant Jemaa el-Fnaa square just a short drive away.

    La Maison Arabe

    Nestled in the Medina, La Maison Arabe is renowned for its luxurious ambiance, featuring elegantly decorated rooms, a serene courtyard, and a renowned cooking school. Its proximity to local markets and attractions makes it a favored choice for travelers seeking an authentic Moroccan experience.

    Equity Point Marrakech

    Located near Jemaa el-Fnaa, Equity Point Marrakech is a vibrant hostel offering both dormitory and private rooms. With a lively atmosphere, rooftop terrace, and organized activities, it's ideal for travelers seeking social engagement and cultural immersion.

    Souvenir Ideas

    Things to remember Marrakesh by:

    Leather Goods

    Marrakesh's souks are renowned for high-quality leather products, including bags, wallets, jackets, and traditional slippers known as babouches.

    Silver Jewelry

    High-quality silk ties, often adorned with traditional Indian patterns and motifs, make for elegant and practical souvenirs. They blend classic style with a touch of Indian artistry. Markets like Dilli Haat are excellent places to find such items.

    Argan Oil

    Argan oil, sourced from the argan tree native to Morocco, is popular for its nourishing properties, used in cosmetics and culinary delights.

    Handwoven Rugs and Carpets

    Marrakesh is known for its handwoven rugs and carpets, each reflecting the city's rich cultural heritage.

    Teapots

    Indulging in Morocco's signature mint tea is a sensory journey; the sweet, aromatic brew is a symbol of hospitality and is traditionally served in ornate teapots. After experiencing this ritual, owning a handcrafted Moroccan teapot becomes a cherished memento, allowing you to recreate the authentic tea experience at home.

    Staying Safe

    To do:

    1: Have Fun

    2: Stay Safe

    Modest Dress

    In Marrakesh, dressing modestly is a sign of respect for local customs and culture. Women are advised to cover their shoulders, chests, and knees. Opt for lightweight, loose-fitting clothing such as long skirts, maxi dresses, or linen trousers paired with long-sleeved tops. This attire helps you blend in with the local community and shows cultural sensitivity.

    Popular Tourist Scams

    Be cautious of individuals offering unsolicited assistance or services, such as guiding you through the souks or taking photos with you, and then demanding high fees. Always agree on prices beforehand and be wary of "free" services that may come with unexpected charges.

    Food and Water Safety

    To avoid foodborne illnesses, eat at reputable establishments where food is prepared hygienically. It's advisable to drink bottled water and avoid ice cubes, as tap water quality can vary.

    Verify Prices Before Paying

    Always confirm the price of goods and services before accepting them. In markets (souks), haggling is customary, but ensure you agree on a final price before the transaction. For taxis, it's best to agree on the fare before starting your journey or ensure the meter is used. This practice helps prevent misunderstandings and ensures fair transactions.

    Tipping Scams

    While tipping is customary, be cautious of individuals who expect tips for unsolicited services or assistance. It's best to offer tips directly to the person providing the service and to be aware of any hidden fees added to bills.

    Stay Informed and Prepared

    • Know Emergency Contacts: Keep a list of emergency contacts, including local authorities and your country's embassy or consulate.
    • Trust Your Instincts: If a situation or area feels uncomfortable, trust your instincts and remove yourself from it. Prioritize your safety at all times.
